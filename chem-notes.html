Covalent/Molecular Bonds

Stability

Atoms are most stable when same valence electrons as noble gas

Two types of bonds

Electronegativity

> 1.7: covalent

< 1.7: molecular

sharing electron

\(\ce{H + Cl}\)

Polarity of Covalent Bonds

Difference in electronegativity:

< 0.4:

shared electron moving between atoms

evenly distributed

1.7 > d > 0.4:

- pulled to one side

- spends much more time on one side of the bond

- partial charges: + side / - side

- - "slanted music note" (lowercase delta ùõø)

Covalent Lewis Diagram

\(\ce{H2} \qquad \ce{H-H}\)

overlapping circles (Venn diagram)


\(\ce{CO2}\)

\(\ce{O=C=O}\)

All atoms have 8 valence electrons

shared electrons = 2 x pairs

Edge Cases

- only shared between two atoms

- only up to triple bonds

- can mix ionic/molecular bonds

Lewis Diagram Steps

1. get total # valence electrons

2. add or subtract electrons if it has a charge (polyatomic ions)

3. put one atom in the center and other atoms around it

4. make one bond between central atom and all atoms around it

4. a) subtract 2 electrons per bond

5. add electrons to outer atoms until full

6. if extra atoms, add to center as lone pairs

6. a) add double/triple bonds if center is still missing

VSEPR Theory

Polarity in Molecules

Overall polarity is based on its shapes and bonds

can put polar bonds to make non-polar bonds

two "trucks" pulling in different directions, a non-polar molecule

Third "truck" pulls in a different direction = uneven pull from different directions = polar

Fourth "truck" pulls in another different direction: even pull = non-polar

Fourth "truck" replaced by a weak car = uneven pull from stronger pull on one side = polar


Rule of Thumb:

If the shape is symmetrical, the molecule will be non-polar

If the shape is asymmetrical, the molecule will be polar.

What is VSEPR Theory?

VSEPR theory (pronounced ves-purr) is used to predict the shapes of molecules based on the repulsion of the bonding and non-bonding electrons in the molecule


Start by drawing Lewis diagram

Treat the multiple bonds as if they were single bonds

Organic Chemistry

Organic molecules are covalently-bonded, made of carbons and hydrogens

Isomers: different organic molecules with the same formula

Acetic acid: \(\ce{C2H3O2-} \text{ or }\ce{CH3COO-}\)


More organic than non-organic molecules

Carbon: 4 valence electrons -> 4 bonds

- can form tetrahedral structures

Readily bonds with other carbons in branches, rings, etc.

Also bonds with \(\ce{O, N, S}\), and halogens


Organic Chemistry Tree

Base -> branches
