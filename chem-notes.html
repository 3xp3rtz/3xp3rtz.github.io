Covalent/Molecular Bonds

Stability

Atoms are most stable when same valence electrons as noble gas

Two types of bonds

Electronegativity

> 1.7: covalent

< 1.7: molecular

sharing electron

\(\ce{H + Cl}\)

Polarity of Covalent Bonds

Difference in electronegativity:

< 0.4:

shared electron moving between atoms

evenly distributed

1.7 > d > 0.4:

- pulled to one side

- spends much more time on one side of the bond

- partial charges: + side / - side

- - "slanted music note" (lowercase delta ùõø)

Edge Cases

- only shared between two atoms

- only up to triple bonds

- can mix ionic/molecular bonds

Lewis Diagram Steps

1. get total # valence electrons

2. add or subtract electrons if it has a charge (polyatomic ions)

3. put one atom in the center and other atoms around it

4. make one bond between central atom and all atoms around it

4. a) subtract 2 electrons per bond

5. add electrons to outer atoms until full

6. if extra atoms, add to center as lone pairs

6. a) add double/triple bonds if center is still missing

VSEPR Theory

Polarity in Molecules

Overall polarity is based on its shapes and bonds

can put polar bonds to make non-polar bonds

two "trucks" pulling in different directions, a non-polar molecule

Third "truck" pulls in a different direction = uneven pull from different directions = polar

Fourth "truck" pulls in another different direction: even pull = non-polar

Fourth "truck" replaced by a weak car = uneven pull from stronger pull on one side = polar


Rule of Thumb:

If the shape is symmetrical, the molecule will be non-polar

If the shape is asymmetrical, the molecule will be polar.

What is VSEPR Theory?

VSEPR theory (pronounced ves-purr) is used to predict the shapes of molecules based on the repulsion of the bonding and non-bonding electrons in the molecule

Forces of Attraction

Intermolecular forces between molecules in gases and liquids

Can be strong or weak

Determine how easy it is for gases to coalesce into liquids, and liquids to solidify

e.g. determines liquids boiling point

Melting point < boiling point


Ionic Attraction

Ionic compounds held together via electrostatic interactions

Form solids with high melting points that are generally water soluble

Water = polar, positive interacts with negative ions, vice versa

High melting points come from bonds to many other formula units

When dissolved in water, form electrolytic solutions

Conductive in liquid state

Covalent Attraction

Gases & liquids: two types of forces

\(\quad\)Intermolecular - Forces of attraction between molecules

\(\qquad\)Weaker than ionic bonds

\(\quad\)Intramolecular - Forces of attraction within molecules

\(\qquad\)Stronger than ionic bonds - 30-400x stronger than inter-

van der Waals forces

Covalent bonds stronger than ionic bonds

Intermolecular Forces of Attraction

1. London Dispersion Force (LDF) (aka induced dipole force)

2. Dipole-Dipole Force

3. Hydrogen Bond

special kind of dipole


Strenghts:

LDF: Weakest (<1 Kcal)

Dipole-Dipole: Stronger (2-5 Kcal)

Hydrogen bond: Strongest (12-16 Kcal)

True covalent bond takes 400Kcal to break


London Dispersion Forces (LDF)

Induced charge: induction

Momentary shift in electron cloud density

The bigger the molecule, the more likely it is to have imbalance; the more likely attraction is

In all molecules: induced charge

The only (predominant) force in non-polar compounds

Non-polar compounds do not have a permanent charge distribution within the molecule

E.g. Iodine vs. Fluorine

Iodine is bigger, more electrons to create imbalance

Iodine has more attraction: solid at room temp

Fluorine has less attraction: gas at room temp


Molecules with even electron distribution are typically:

1. single atoms

2. same element molecules

3. hydrocarbons

4. symmetrical molecules

E.g. Ne, Kr, H2, O2, N2, S8, C3H8, CCl4, CO2


LDF gets stronger the larger the atom/molecule

large electron clouds more difficult to deform

Larger molecule = higher boiling point

Propane vs. Butane


Dipole-Dipole Forces

Permanent dipoles = polar (positive and negative sides)

Stronger than LDFs (<1 Kcal vs. 2-5 Kcal)

e.g. HCl


Hydrogen Bonds

Dipole that includes hydrogen

Special form of Dipole-Dipole, not a true chemical bond

Strongest intermolecular force

Much stronger than Dipole-Dipole & LDF

Only exists between H and N/O/F

- High electronegativity, small atoms

- Polar bond (large difference in electronegativity)

H + other atoms = regular Dipole-Dipole

Hydrogen is ALWAYS the + dipole, N/O/F is ALWAYS - dipole

Large differences in polarity make strong "bonds"

H + F strongest (difference in e.n.)

F > O > N


Examples with Hydrogen Bonds

Water (\(\ce{H2O}\)), ethyl/methyl alcohol, \(\ce{HF, NH3}\)

Water is the only substance whose solid is less dense than its liquid

Water aligns into hexagonal structures


Takes more energy to move molecules from liquid to solid

Molecules of similar molecular weight without ability to form H-bonds have lower B.P

Smaller molecules should lead to lower boiling points

More hydrogen bonds usually leads to higher boiling points

Limiting reagent

The reactant which determines the amount of product that can be formed in a product; the reagent which runs out before all the others

NaCl can be prepared with sodium metal + chlorine gas

\(\ce{2Na_{(s)} + Cl2_{(g)} -> 2NaCl_{(s)}}\)

Comparing Reactants

mol Cl2 = mol Na x mol Cl2/mol Na = 6.70 mol Na x 1 mol Cl2/2 mol Na = 3.35 mol Cl2 Since we have 3.20 mol Cl2, we do not have enough, therefore Cl2 is the limiting reagent.

Comparing Products

mol NaCl = mol Na x mol NaCl/mol Na = 6.70 mol x 2 mol NaCl/2 mol Na = 6.70 mol NaCl mol NaCl = mol Cl2 x mol NaCl/mol Cl2 = 3.20 mol Cl2 x 2 mol NaCl/mol Cl2 = 6.40 mol Since Cl2 makes less NaCl, it is the limiting factor. Percent Yield

Theoretical yield: the maximum amount of product that could possibly be formed from given amounts of reactants

Actual yield: the product that actually forms when the reaction is carried out in the lab; is often less than theoretical yield

Percent yield: the ratio of the actual yield to the theoretical yield in percent. Measures the efficiency of the reaction

\(\text{percent yield} = \frac{\text{actual yield}}{\text{theoretical yield}}\times 100\)

Solutions

Solute + solvent -> solution

Solute: the thing that is dissolved

Solvent: substance that dissolves the solute; almost always in higher quantity than solute

Solution: homogeneous mixture of solvent and 1+ solute(s)


Solutions can have variable composition - different ratios of solvent:solute possible

Dilute (Weak) solutions: low density of solute in solvent

Concentrated (Strong) solutions: high density of solute in solvent

Missible: can dissolve in any proportion

Aqueous solutions: water is solvent

Types of Solutes

- Ionic compounds: compounds with ionic bonds (polar water can interact with ionic)

- Polar molecules: molecule with an uneven distribution of charge; one end has a partial +- charge

- Non-polar molecules: covalently-bonded molecule that does not possess a partial charge

- mostly do not dissolve in water, with some exceptions (very small non-polars)

- CO2

- O2


Solubility

The amount of a solute that dissolves in a given quality of solvent at a specific temp.

e.g. solubility of NaCl in water at 20*C = 36g/100mL


Saturated solution: a solution where no more of a solute can be dissolved in it (at a specific temp.)

Unsaturated solution: a solution which can dissolve more of a solute (at a specific temp.)

Supersaturated solution: heated solution, dissolved more of it, very slowly cooled down; usually very unstable

- Honey: a supersaturated solution


Soluble: solubility > 1g/100mL of solvent

Insoluble: solubility < 0.1g/100mL of solvent

Slightly soluble: 0.1g/100mL < solubility < 1g/100mL of solvent

*does not apply to gases, only solids/liquids in solution

Factors that Affect Rate of Dissolving vs. Solubility

How fast something dissolves \(\neq\) how much of something dissolves

Affect Rate of Dissolving

1. Temperature

- increased rate of movement -> increased collisions

2. Agitation

- shaking, stirring, etc.

- more contact between solute/solvent

3. Particle size

- smaller size -> larger surface area


Affect Solubility

1. Molecule size

- larger size -> lower solubility

2. Temperature

- Solids - higher temperature -> more energy to break intermolecular bonds -> higher solubility

- Gas - higher temperature -> more energy in gas molecules, require losing energy to dissolve -> lower solubility

3. Pressure

- Gas - increased pressure on gas -> more solubility
